Questions

1. What were the key events and relationships that led up to the inappropriate release? Visualize the sequence of events (key actions, causal relationships, decision points and actors, and the decisions/system elements that let the post past embargo enforcement).

AgentFlow anchors the curated causal chain E01–E12 directly onto each actor's thread in the Behavioral Drift Braid (Fig. 1.1) and lays it out in time order in the Critical Path (Fig. 1.2). The sequence reads: the CEO privately seeds “strategic developments” (May 17, E01) → a covert side_huddle channel is activated and the merger briefing is held there (May 22, E02–E03) → The Judge is assigned only after @Elena's public faux pas (May 30, E04) → external pressure from the SaltWind exposés (May 31 / Jun 4, E05–E06) → on June 5 an anonymous post references the deal (09:49, E07), boundary tests escalate (E08), The Judge issues a ceiling warning at 15:08 (E09) that is ignored → Legal posts on its personal account at 17:25 (E10), Social-Media amplifies (E11), and the breach is confirmed before 18:00 (E12). The key decision points are: activating the covert channel, holding the briefing there, ignoring E09, and the final decision to post.

Four system elements let the post past enforcement, all visible on the braid: (1) late control — Judge gates only appear from May 30, after coordination had gone covert; (2) the “JUDGE SILENT” band — zero compliance output during the crisis-morning hours when the first violations occurred; (3) advisory-only power — the 15:08 warning is a ruling, not a block; (4) scope — enforcement watched the official account, while the breach travelled through the personal and anonymous channels, exactly where the Legal and Social-Media threads end the day.

2. The evasion of the embargo was a new behavior. Provide visual analytics that discover and illustrate the typical behavior of systems/agents/people involved. How does the behavior that led to the release compare to the prior behaviors?

Typical behavior is defined visually: each agent's dotted baseline is the mean (±σ) of its communication posture over the pre-crisis rounds, drawn on the same ladder the threads travel (Fig. 2.1). For two weeks every thread holds flat in the monitored/private zones; posture is computed per round as the channel average with public messages weighted ×3, so any public exposure visibly pulls a thread down.

On June 5 the behavior breaks pattern in a way the braid makes literal: the Legal thread dives across the red enforcement perimeter into zones it had never occupied. Its behavioral profile (Fig. 2.2) quantifies the deviation: L1 deviation 0.31, 176 crisis-day messages vs. a 90-message baseline, 12 anonymous posts, 47 covert messages, and — the core anomaly — two public channels never used in baseline (anonymous and personal, flagged “NEW” in the profile). Social-Media mirrors the dive; Quality, PR and the interns stay near baseline, isolating the anomaly to exactly two actors. The same two agents dominate the covert channel (restricted betweenness: Legal 0.36; brokerage: Social-Media 1.00, Legal 0.78), i.e., the internal→public conduit and the breach executors are the same pair.

3. Were there leading indicators that such a release was possible? (prior expected-vs-actual deviations; other occasions with breach-like behaviors; why prior occasions produced no action)

Leading indicators — yes. The seismograph (Fig. 3.1) scores every round with a composite anomaly (lexical sensitivity ×0.4 + covert messages ×1 + boundary tests ×3 + violations ×6) against pre-crisis μ+1σ/μ+2σ thresholds. It exceeds +1σ on May 22 (covert-channel activation) and May 29 (public near-incident), and crosses +2σ hours before the breach; anonymous pre-seeding starts at 09:49 on June 5, eight hours before the confirmed breach.

Prior deviations. May 22: two senior agents move merger talk off the monitored channel (first posture drop; purple co-participation arcs). May 29: @Elena's public post skirts merger language — the faux pas that triggered The Judge's assignment. May 31–Jun 4: governance-framed public “clarifications” rise — each locally defensible, jointly disclosing.

Similar episodes. The breach pattern (covert alignment → public boundary test) rehearses twice at smaller scale: May 22–23 (covert cluster + merger-adjacent public post) and May 29–30 (near-incident + defensive clarifications). Brushing the time window to May makes both rehearsals visually isomorphic to June 5 (Fig. 3.2).

Why no action. Every green Judge gate was read as success (May 30–Jun 4 containments bred false confidence); each boundary test was individually defensible, so rule-based alerts never fired; and the only escalation mechanism was advisory. The system punished blunt violations but was blind to the gradient — which is precisely what the composite score makes visible.

Reflection Questions (Optional)

1. What was the most difficult part of working on this year’s data? What could have made it more accessible?

The hardest part was that the ground truth is distributed and graded: no single record is “the leak,” so naive filtering finds nothing and full reading is infeasible. We had to engineer intermediate, auditable signals (lexicon families — especially the “governance” cover vocabulary — posture, coordination windows, restricted betweenness) before any visualization was meaningful. Irregular sampling (daily, then hourly) also breaks linear timelines; an ordinal round scale was required. A machine-readable event/channel codebook and explicit channel-visibility semantics in the data dictionary would have made the dataset considerably more accessible.
